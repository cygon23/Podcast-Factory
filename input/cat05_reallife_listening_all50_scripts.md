## Lesson 1: Job Interview | مقابلة عمل

**Scenario:** Sara applies for a marketing coordinator position at a mid-sized company. The interviewer, Mr. Blake, conducts a professional interview.

**Host Intro:**
Welcome to Dorosak English Podcast! Category 5 — Real-life Listening, Lesson 1. Today you'll hear a realistic job interview. Listen carefully to the questions, the structure, and how Sara answers confidently.

---

**Mr. Blake:** Good morning, Sara. Thank you for coming in today. Please, have a seat.
**Sara:** Good morning, Mr. Blake. Thank you for having me. I'm really excited about this opportunity.
**Mr. Blake:** Excellent. Let's start with the basics — could you tell me a little about yourself?
**Sara:** Of course. I graduated three years ago with a degree in Communications. Since then, I've worked at two marketing agencies where I specialised in digital campaigns and social media strategy.
**Mr. Blake:** Impressive. And what draws you to this particular role?
**Sara:** I've followed your company's growth closely, and I admire how you blend creativity with data-driven decisions. I believe my skills align well with that approach.
**Mr. Blake:** Good answer. Can you describe a time you handled a difficult project under pressure?
**Sara:** Certainly. Last year, a client campaign went over budget mid-launch. I restructured the spending plan, communicated clearly with the client, and we still delivered on time and within 5% of the original budget.
**Mr. Blake:** That's exactly the kind of problem-solving we value. Do you have any questions for us?
**Sara:** Yes — what does success look like in this role in the first six months?
**Mr. Blake:** Great question. We'd want you fully integrated with the team and leading your first independent campaign. We'll discuss further. Thank you, Sara — we'll be in touch.
**Sara:** Thank you so much, Mr. Blake. I look forward to hearing from you.

**Key Listening Points:**
- How to open an interview confidently
- Describing experience clearly
- Answering the "difficult project" question using the STAR method
- Asking a thoughtful question at the end

## Lesson 2: Teacher-Student Conversation | حوار بين معلم وطالب

**Scenario:** A university student, James, visits his professor, Dr. Patel, during office hours to discuss a disappointing grade and ask for advice.

**Host Intro:**
Lesson 2 — A teacher-student conversation. This is a very common real-life interaction. Listen to how James approaches his professor respectfully and how Dr. Patel responds constructively.

---

**James:** *knocks* Dr. Patel? Do you have a moment? I wanted to talk about my essay grade.
**Dr. Patel:** Of course, James. Come in and sit down. I was actually expecting you.
**James:** I was a bit surprised by the mark, to be honest. I thought I'd covered all the main points.
**Dr. Patel:** You did cover the main points — but covering them isn't quite the same as analysing them deeply. That's what this level requires.
**James:** I see. Could you show me where I went wrong specifically?
**Dr. Patel:** Absolutely. Look here — your argument is clear, but you haven't engaged with the counterargument at all. In academic writing, you need to acknowledge the opposing view and then refute it.
**James:** Right, I skipped that entirely. I thought it would weaken my argument.
**Dr. Patel:** Actually, it strengthens it. It shows you've thought critically. Also, your conclusion simply restates the introduction — it needs to synthesise your findings.
**James:** This is really helpful. Is there anything I can do to improve before the final submission?
**Dr. Patel:** I'd suggest rewriting the conclusion and adding a counterargument paragraph. Bring it to me before Friday and I'll give you feedback.
**James:** That's very kind of you. Thank you, Dr. Patel.
**Dr. Patel:** That's what I'm here for. Keep pushing — you have potential.

**Key Listening Points:**
- Polite way to question a grade
- Academic vocabulary: analyse, counterargument, refute, synthesise
- How professors give constructive feedback
- How to ask for a second chance professionally

## Lesson 3: Business Meeting | اجتماع عمل

**Scenario:** A weekly team meeting at a marketing firm. Manager Linda runs the meeting with her team: Tom, Sara, and Dev.

**Host Intro:**
Lesson 3 — A business meeting. Meetings are a core part of professional life. Listen to the structure, vocabulary, and how people take turns speaking in this realistic office meeting.

---

**Linda:** Good morning, everyone. Let's get started — we've got a full agenda today. Tom, can you give us a quick update on the client campaign?
**Tom:** Sure. We're on track for the Tuesday launch. The visuals are finalised and copy is with the client for final approval.
**Linda:** Great. Any blockers?
**Tom:** One — we're waiting on the social media access from their end. I'll chase that today.
**Linda:** Good. Sara, what's the status on the analytics dashboard?
**Sara:** It's 80% complete. We've hit a small technical issue with the data feeds, but Dev is working on a fix.
**Dev:** Should be resolved by end of day, Linda. Worst case, tomorrow morning.
**Linda:** Okay. Make sure we flag it if it slips further. We can't launch without it. Right — any other business?
**Tom:** Quick one — the client has asked about adding a video component. Is that in scope?
**Linda:** That's a scope change, so it would need a new quote. Tom, draft a proposal and we'll review it in Thursday's call.
**Tom:** Will do.
**Linda:** Good work, team. Let's keep the momentum going. Meeting adjourned — I'll send the minutes by noon.

**Key Listening Points:**
- How to run and participate in a meeting
- Business vocabulary: agenda, blockers, flag, in scope, minutes
- How to give status updates concisely
- Asking and handling scope changes

## Lesson 4: Visiting the Doctor | زيارة الطبيب

**Scenario:** Patient Maria visits her GP, Dr. Khan, with symptoms she's been experiencing for a week.

**Host Intro:**
Lesson 4 — Visiting the doctor. This is one of the most important real-life conversations you'll ever have. Listen carefully to how Maria describes her symptoms and how Dr. Khan responds.

---

**Receptionist:** Next patient, please. Maria Thompson?
**Maria:** That's me.
**Dr. Khan:** Maria, come on in. Take a seat. So, what brings you in today?
**Maria:** I've been feeling really run-down for about a week now. I have a constant headache, my throat is sore, and I've been exhausted even after sleeping.
**Dr. Khan:** I see. Any fever or chills?
**Maria:** A mild fever, yes. Around 37.8 most mornings.
**Dr. Khan:** Any difficulty swallowing? Or swollen glands?
**Maria:** A little, yes — my neck feels a bit tender here.
**Dr. Khan:** Let me have a look. *examines* Yes, your glands are slightly swollen and your throat is quite red. I think this could be a viral infection — possibly the early stages of glandular fever. I'd like to run a blood test to confirm.
**Maria:** Is that serious?
**Dr. Khan:** It's manageable. But if it is glandular fever, you'll need plenty of rest and we'll monitor your liver function. In the meantime, take paracetamol for the fever, drink lots of fluids, and avoid alcohol.
**Maria:** Understood. How long before I feel better?
**Dr. Khan:** Could be one to three weeks. Come back if it worsens. The blood test results will be back within 48 hours.
**Maria:** Thank you, Doctor.

**Key Listening Points:**
- Describing symptoms clearly
- Medical vocabulary: glands, viral infection, liver function, paracetamol
- Asking follow-up questions at the doctor
- Understanding doctor's instructions

## Lesson 5: Tourist Guide | مرشد سياحي

**Scenario:** Tour guide Elena leads a group of tourists around the historic old town of a European city.

**Host Intro:**
Lesson 5 — A tourist guide tour. Listen to Elena's vivid, engaging narration and notice how she engages her group, shares facts, and handles questions.

---

**Elena:** Good morning, everyone! Welcome to the old town of Tallinn — one of the best-preserved medieval cities in Northern Europe. I'm Elena, your guide today. We have about two hours together, so please stay close and feel free to ask questions anytime.
**Tourist 1:** How old is this city?
**Elena:** Great question! The old town dates back to the 13th century, making it over 800 years old. If you look to your left, you'll see the Town Hall — that building alone has stood since 1402. Isn't that incredible?
**Tourist 2:** Can we go inside?
**Elena:** Absolutely! We'll stop there in about twenty minutes. Now, follow me through this archway — and watch your step, the cobblestones can be slippery.
**Tourist 3:** What's that tower called?
**Elena:** That's Hermann Tower — the tallest point in the old town. The Estonian flag has flown from the top since independence. It's a powerful national symbol.
**Tourist 1:** This place is breathtaking. Has it always been so well preserved?
**Elena:** Largely, yes. The old town was protected during Soviet times because the government used the buildings. It actually worked in the city's favour. Now it's a UNESCO World Heritage Site.
**Tourist 2:** What do you recommend for lunch around here?
**Elena:** Oh, I have the perfect place — a little cellar restaurant serving traditional Estonian food. Dark bread, elk soup, and the best apple cake you'll ever taste.
**Tourist 3:** Count me in!
**Elena:** Ha! Right this way, everyone. Step into history!

**Key Listening Points:**
- How tour guides structure their narration
- Historical and cultural vocabulary
- Handling tourist questions smoothly
- Enthusiastic and engaging tone

## Lesson 6: University Debate | مناظرة جامعية

**Scenario:** A university debate on the motion: "Social media does more harm than good." Two students — Lena (for) and Kai (against) — present their arguments.

**Host Intro:**
Lesson 6 — A university debate. Academic debates have a specific structure and vocabulary. Listen to how Lena and Kai present opposing arguments confidently and respectfully.

---

**Moderator:** Welcome to today's debate. The motion is: "Social media does more harm than good." Lena will argue in favour of the motion. Kai will argue against. Lena, you have two minutes.

**Lena:** Thank you. I firmly believe that social media, despite its benefits, is doing considerable damage to our society. Studies show that excessive use leads to anxiety, depression, and a distorted sense of reality — particularly in young people. The algorithms are designed to keep us scrolling, not to keep us healthy. Furthermore, misinformation spreads faster on social media than facts ever could. The harm is measurable and growing. Thank you.

**Moderator:** Thank you, Lena. Kai, your response.

**Kai:** With respect to my opponent, I believe the motion oversimplifies a complex issue. Social media has connected billions of people, given voice to the marginalised, and accelerated social movements for justice and equality. The Arab Spring, #MeToo, climate activism — none of these would have reached global scale without social media. The problem is not the platforms themselves, but how they are regulated. We should not ban the tool — we should govern it better. Thank you.

**Moderator:** Rebuttals. Lena?

**Lena:** Governance requires political will that simply doesn't exist. Meanwhile, real damage is happening to real people, right now.

**Kai:** And innovation always carries risk. We didn't ban cars because of accidents — we introduced seatbelts. The answer is smart policy, not fear.

**Moderator:** A fascinating exchange. We'll now open to the floor for questions.

**Key Listening Points:**
- Formal debate structure and language
- How to argue a point with evidence
- Rebuttal techniques
- Academic vocabulary: motion, opponent, marginalised, rebuttal, governance

## Lesson 7: Giving a Presentation | تقديم عرض تقديمي

**Scenario:** Marketing analyst Priya delivers a quarterly results presentation to her company's leadership team.

**Host Intro:**
Lesson 7 — Giving a presentation. Professional presentations have a clear structure. Listen to how Priya opens, presents data, and handles questions with confidence.

---

**Priya:** Good afternoon, everyone. Thank you for making the time. Today I'll be walking you through our Q3 marketing performance — what worked, what didn't, and where we go from here. I'll keep it to fifteen minutes and save time for questions at the end.

So, to start with the headline number — our overall reach grew by 34% this quarter. That's our strongest quarter in two years. *clicks slide* As you can see here, email campaigns drove 45% of that growth, significantly outperforming social media for the third consecutive quarter.

Moving on to what didn't work — our paid search spend increased by 20%, but conversions dropped by 8%. This suggests a targeting issue, not a budget issue. We're reviewing the keyword strategy and I expect to have a revised plan by next Friday.

Looking ahead to Q4, our three priorities are: first, optimising paid search; second, launching the new loyalty campaign we've been piloting; and third, strengthening our brand presence ahead of the holiday season.

To summarise — strong growth, one area for improvement, and a clear plan forward. I'm confident in our targets for Q4.

I'll take questions now. Yes — Sarah?

**Sarah:** What's driving the email campaign success specifically?
**Priya:** Great question. Personalisation. We segmented the audience far more granularly this quarter and it made a significant difference. Happy to share the breakdown after this.

**Key Listening Points:**
- Presentation structure: intro, data, issues, outlook, summary
- Business vocabulary: headline number, conversions, segmented, granularly
- How to handle questions at the end
- Confident, clear delivery style

## Lesson 8: Conference Discussion | مناقشة في مؤتمر

**Scenario:** At an international education conference, panellists Dr. Rowe, Ms. Chen, and Mr. Ali discuss the future of online learning.

**Host Intro:**
Lesson 8 — A conference panel discussion. Conferences bring together experts to share ideas. Listen to the discussion and notice how experts exchange views professionally.

---

**Moderator:** Welcome to our panel: "The Future of Education — Digital or Human?" I'd like to open with a question for Dr. Rowe. Has online learning lived up to its promise?

**Dr. Rowe:** Partially. The pandemic accelerated adoption dramatically, and we discovered that online learning can work well for self-motivated learners. However, it's exposed a significant equity gap — students without reliable internet or quiet study spaces have suffered.

**Moderator:** Ms. Chen, your research focuses on student engagement. What are you seeing?

**Ms. Chen:** The data is nuanced. Engagement dips significantly in purely asynchronous environments. What works best is a hybrid model — structured in-person sessions combined with flexible online content. Neither alone is sufficient.

**Moderator:** Mr. Ali, you represent a developing-country perspective. How does this discussion land from where you sit?

**Mr. Ali:** With some frustration, to be honest. We are still discussing infrastructure while the conversation in the West has already moved to AI tutors. The priorities couldn't be more different. For many of our students, a stable school building is still the dream — not a virtual classroom.

**Dr. Rowe:** That's a vital point. The global conversation needs to account for that reality more honestly.

**Moderator:** A sobering reminder. Let's open to the floor — any questions for our panel?

**Key Listening Points:**
- How panels are structured
- Academic register: equity gap, asynchronous, hybrid model, infrastructure
- How to express different perspectives respectfully
- Listening for agreement and disagreement between speakers

## Lesson 9: Phone Conversation | محادثة هاتفية

**Scenario:** Jake calls his friend Lily on the phone. They have a natural, casual phone conversation about weekend plans.

**Host Intro:**
Lesson 9 — A phone conversation between friends. Phone conversations have their own rhythm and expressions. Listen carefully — you won't see any body language, so the words carry everything.

---

**Lily:** Hello?
**Jake:** Lily! Hey, it's Jake. Am I catching you at a bad time?
**Lily:** Jake! No, not at all — I was just watching TV. What's up?
**Jake:** Not much. I was calling to see if you're free this Saturday?
**Lily:** This Saturday… let me think. Yeah, I should be free. What did you have in mind?
**Jake:** A bunch of us are thinking of going to that new rooftop bar in the city. You in?
**Lily:** Ooh, that sounds fun! What time?
**Jake:** Around seven. We'd probably grab dinner first somewhere nearby — haven't figured that out yet.
**Lily:** I'm definitely in for the bar. Not sure about dinner — I might have something earlier. Can I let you know?
**Jake:** Of course, no pressure. Just message me by Friday so I can tell the others.
**Lily:** Perfect. Who else is coming?
**Jake:** Me, Dan, Sophie, and maybe Tom — he's not confirmed yet.
**Lily:** Love that group. Okay, I'll be there for sure. Send me the details when you have them?
**Jake:** Will do! Oh, and can you remind Dan? He never checks his messages.
**Lily:** Ha! Classic Dan. Sure, I'll give him a nudge.
**Jake:** Brilliant. Alright, I'll let you get back to your TV. See you Saturday!
**Lily:** Can't wait. Speak soon, Jake!

**Key Listening Points:**
- Natural phone conversation flow
- Casual expressions: What's up, You in?, give him a nudge
- How to confirm plans and leave things open
- Friendly tone and natural closings

## Lesson 11: Restaurant Service Dialogue | حوار في مطعم

**Scenario:** A family of three visits a restaurant. The waiter, Michael, manages their table from seating to payment.

**Host Intro:**
Lesson 11 — A restaurant service dialogue. Restaurants are one of the most common real-life English settings. Listen to the full experience from arrival to paying the bill.

---

**Michael:** Good evening! Welcome to La Maison. Do you have a reservation?
**Dad:** Yes — under Carter, for three.
**Michael:** Perfect, Mr. Carter. Right this way. *leads to table* Here are your menus. Tonight's specials are on the insert. Can I get you some drinks while you decide?
**Mum:** I'll have a glass of the house white, please.
**Dad:** Sparkling water for me.
**Child:** Can I have orange juice?
**Michael:** Of course. I'll be right back. *returns* Here you are. Are you ready to order?
**Mum:** Yes — I'll have the salmon, please. Medium rare.
**Dad:** The ribeye for me, well done. And could we have some bread for the table?
**Michael:** Absolutely. And for the young one?
**Child:** Pasta with tomato sauce, please.
**Michael:** Lovely choices. Any starters?
**Dad:** The soup of the day — what is it tonight?
**Michael:** Butternut squash with crème fraîche.
**Dad:** We'll take two of those, please.
**Michael:** *later* How is everything tasting?
**Mum:** Delicious, thank you. Could I get some extra lemon for the salmon?
**Michael:** Right away. *at the end* Was everything to your satisfaction?
**Dad:** Wonderful, thank you. Could we have the bill?
**Michael:** Of course. Was it one bill or separate?
**Dad:** One, please.

**Key Listening Points:**
- Full restaurant dialogue from arrival to payment
- How to order food precisely
- Waiter vocabulary: specials, starters, to your satisfaction

## Lesson 14: Weather Forecast | نشرة جوية

**Scenario:** Meteorologist David presents a detailed five-day weather forecast for the UK on a morning programme.

**Host Intro:**
Lesson 14 — A weather forecast. Weather forecasts use specific vocabulary and a clear geographical structure. Listen to how David describes conditions across different regions.

---

**David:** Good morning. Here's your five-day forecast for the UK.

Starting with today — it's going to be a mixed picture across the country. Scotland will see some bright spells in the morning, but cloud will build through the afternoon and there's a chance of light showers by this evening, particularly around Glasgow and Edinburgh. Temperatures there sitting around fourteen degrees.

Moving into England — the north will be largely overcast with patchy rain at times. The Midlands should see some sunny intervals by midday, making it the warmer part of the country today at around eighteen degrees. Down in the south and southeast, it's looking pleasant — largely dry with plenty of sunshine and highs of twenty-one in London.

Wales will be cool and breezy, with some coastal winds picking up this afternoon. Northern Ireland — a mostly cloudy day, but dry on the whole.

Looking ahead to tomorrow — a weather front will push in from the west overnight, bringing widespread rain across most of the UK by Wednesday morning. Heavy at times in the northwest. Thursday and Friday are looking drier and warmer, with temperatures recovering to the low twenties by the weekend.

So — umbrella handy for mid-week, but brighter days ahead. I'll be back at noon with an update. Back to you in the studio.

**Key Listening Points:**
- Weather vocabulary: bright spells, overcast, patchy rain, sunny intervals, weather front
- Geographical structure: Scotland, England, Wales, Northern Ireland
- Describing forecasts for different time periods
- Formal-but-friendly broadcast register

## Lesson 16: Customer Complaint | شكوى من عميل

**Scenario:** An angry customer, Mr. Webb, calls a telecoms company to complain about his broadband service. Agent Donna handles the call.

**Host Intro:**
Lesson 16 — A customer complaint. Complaints are common in everyday life. Listen to how Mr. Webb expresses his frustration and how Donna handles it professionally.

---

**Donna:** Good afternoon, TechConnect customer service, Donna speaking. How can I help?
**Mr. Webb:** Yes, hello. I've been a customer with you for six years and frankly, the service I've received this week has been absolutely unacceptable.
**Donna:** I'm very sorry to hear that, Mr.—?
**Mr. Webb:** Webb. Robert Webb. My broadband has been cutting out every day this week — sometimes for hours. I work from home. This is costing me money.
**Donna:** Mr. Webb, I completely understand how disruptive that must be. I sincerely apologise. Let me pull up your account. *pause* I can see there have been several connection drops — you're right. I'm sorry this wasn't flagged to you sooner.
**Mr. Webb:** I had to call you three times. Three times! And every time I was told it was my router.
**Donna:** And was the router replaced?
**Mr. Webb:** Yes, last Tuesday — and it's still cutting out. So it's not the router, is it?

**Mr. Webb:** I'd like some kind of compensation for this week.

**Mr. Webb:** Right. I'm holding you to that.
**Donna:** Of course. I've logged everything. You'll receive a confirmation by email. Thank you for your patience, Mr. Webb.
**Mr. Webb:** Let's hope it gets sorted this time.

**Key Listening Points:**

## Lesson 17: Manager-Staff Meeting | اجتماع بين المدير والموظفين

**Scenario:** Manager Rachel holds a performance review meeting with team member Joe. It's honest, constructive, and professional.

**Host Intro:**
Lesson 17 — A manager and staff member meeting. Performance reviews are a real and important part of working life. Listen to how Rachel gives both positive and critical feedback professionally.

---

**Rachel:** Joe, thanks for coming in. I wanted this to be a two-way conversation, so please feel free to share your own thoughts throughout. How do you think the past quarter has gone for you?
**Joe:** Honestly? Mixed. I'm proud of the client presentations — I felt really on top of those. But I've struggled a bit with time management on the back-end reporting.
**Rachel:** I appreciate your honesty. That mirrors what I've observed. Your client work has been excellent — really excellent. The feedback we've received has been overwhelmingly positive.

## Lesson 49: Celebrity Interview | مقابلة مع مشهور

**Scenario:** Magazine journalist Kate interviews rising film star Leo Davis about his new movie, his career, and his personal life.

**Host Intro:**
Lesson 49 — A celebrity interview. Celebrity interviews combine PR language with personal insight. Listen to how Leo manages questions with charm and openness.

---

**Kate:** Leo, congratulations on Midnight Signal — it's already getting Oscar buzz. How does that feel?
**Leo:** Honestly? Surreal. When I was making it, I just wanted to do justice to the story. The response has gone beyond anything I imagined.
**Kate:** Your performance has been called "career-defining." Does that phrase scare you?
**Leo:** *laughs* A little! I try not to read too much of my own press. It can mess with your head. What I can say is that this role pushed me in ways I hadn't been pushed before.

## Lesson 50: Graduation Ceremony Speech | خطاب حفل التخرج

**Scenario:** University president Professor Diana Wells delivers the commencement address at the annual graduation ceremony.

**Host Intro:**
Lesson 50 — The final lesson in Category 5! A graduation speech. Commencement speeches are some of the most inspiring forms of public speaking. Listen to Professor Wells and absorb every word.

---

**Professor Wells:** Honoured guests, proud families, devoted faculty — and most importantly, the graduating class of this year.

You did it.

*applause*

I want you to hold this moment. Not for long — life will pull you forward soon enough. But just for a breath — acknowledge what it took to get here. The late nights. The doubt. The moments you questioned everything. And then look around at the people beside you — the friends, the peers, the colleagues who walked this journey alongside you. This is your community. Hold onto it.

You graduate into a world that is more complex, more connected, and more uncertain than any generation before you has faced. And I will not stand here and pretend otherwise. But here is what I know: complexity is not the enemy of progress. It is its engine. Every great advance in human history has come from people who looked at a complicated world and said — "I can help with this."

You have been trained not just to accumulate knowledge, but to question it. Not just to follow systems, but to improve them. Use that. Use it generously, and use it bravely.

One final thought. In the years ahead, you will succeed — and you will fail. You will lead — and you will need to be led. Through all of it, remember this: the measure of a person is not what they achieve alone, but what they lift into being for others.

Go. Build. Create. Serve.

Congratulations, Class of this year. The world is genuinely waiting for you.

*applause*

**Key Listening Points:**
- Commencement speech vocabulary: graduating class, faculty, commencement, generation, progress, generously
- Rhetorical structure: acknowledgement, challenge, inspiration, call to action
- Powerful use of short sentences for impact: "You did it." "Go. Build. Create. Serve."
- Emotional register: warm, serious, inspirational
